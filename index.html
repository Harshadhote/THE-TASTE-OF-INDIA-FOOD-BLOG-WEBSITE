Welcome To The Taste Of India

Indian food is different from rest of the world not only in taste but also in cooking methods. It reflects a perfect blend of various cultures and ages. Just like Indian culture, food in India has also been influenced by various civilizations, which have contributed their share in its overall development.
One of the most distinctive features of traditional Indian food is spices. Indian cuisine is known for its bold and complex flavours due to the frequent use of spices, such as turmeric, cumin, coriander, ginger, and black pepper. These spices add a one-of-a-kind taste and have health benefits, mainly anti-inflammatory. Another important aspect of Indian food culture is the use of herbs. Fresh herbs, such as cilantro, mint, and parsley, are commonly used in Indian dishes to add a burst of flavour and A large selection of commonly used Indian cooking spices in wooden trays on an old table. Spices include, chili ,cumin, tumeric, corriander, cumin, cardamon, garam masala, saffron, cinamon and star anise, poppy seed, sesame, cloves, nutmeg, black and white pepper corns, aniseed and fenugreek.






These herbs are also believed to have medicinal properties and are often used in Ayurvedic medicine.The use of legumes, such as lentils, chickpeas, and kidney beans, is also a staple of Indian cuisine. These ingredients are a great source of protein and fibre, and they are often used in vegetarian and vegan dishes.Indian cuisine is also known for its wide variety of bread types, such as naan, roti, and paratha bread. These types are often made with whole wheat flour, and they are served with a variety of curries and stews.

4.West Indian Recipes

In western India, the desert cuisine is famous for its unique taste and varieties of food. Rajasthan and Gujarat are the states that represent the dessert flavor of Indian food. Here an immense variety of dals and achars (pickles/preserves) is used that simply substitutes the relative lack of fresh vegetables in these areas..

In the states like Maharashtra, the food is usually a mix of both north as well as south cooking styles. Here people use both the rice and the wheat with same interest. Along the coastline of Mumbai a wide variety of fishes is available. Some of the delicious preparations include dishes like the Bombay Prawn and Pompfret.

In Goa, that is further down towards south, one can notice Portuguese influence in the cooking style as well as in the dishes. Some of the major dishes of this region are the sweet and sour Vindaloo, duck baffad, sorpotel and egg molie etc.

Cooking Tips

Master the Basics

  • Knife Skills: Learn to properly hold and use a knife. Practicing basic cuts like dicing, slicing, and chopping will speed up your prep time.
  • Mise en Place: This French term means "everything in its place." Prepare and organize your ingredients before you start cooking to streamline the process.

Cooking Techniques

  • Browning Meat: Ensure your pan is hot before adding meat to achieve a good sear, which locks in juices and adds flavor.
  • Deglazing: After browning meat, deglaze the pan with wine, broth, or water to incorporate the flavorful bits stuck to the bottom into your sauce.

Baking Tips

  • Room Temperature Ingredients: For baking, use room temperature ingredients like eggs, butter, and milk. This helps them blend more easily and evenly.
  • Measure Accurately: Baking is a science. Use proper measuring cups and spoons, and follow the recipe measurements exactly.

Healthy Cooking

  • Steam Vegetables: Steaming preserves the nutrients in vegetables better than boiling. It also helps maintain their texture and flavor.
  • Use Less Oil: Opt for non-stick pans or use cooking spray to reduce the amount of oil needed in your recipes.

Safety Tips

  • Proper Food Storage: Store leftovers in airtight containers and refrigerate them promptly. Label containers with the date to keep track of freshness.
  • Knife Safety: Keep your knives sharp. Dull knives require more force to cut, increasing the risk of slipping and cutting yourself.

Time-Saving Hacks

  • Pre-Chopped Ingredients: Use pre-chopped vegetables or frozen fruits and veggies to save prep time without sacrificing nutrition.
  • Slow Cooker: Use a slow cooker to prepare meals overnight or while you're at work. This is great for soups, stews, and braised dishes.

Experiment and Enjoy

  • Try New Recipes: Dont be afraid to experiment with new recipes and ingredients. Its a great way to expand your culinary skills and discover new favorite dishes.
  • Have Fun: Cooking should be enjoyable, not stressful. Play some music, take your time, and enjoy the process of creating delicious meals.

Cultural Stories

Story of khichdi

Khichdi is often considered the ultimate comfort food in India, symbolizing simplicity, nourishment, and warmth. This humble dish of rice and lentils has a rich history that reflects the diverse culinary traditions of India. And the khichdi is one of the famous dish all over the India The staple khichdi made up of rice and lentils is actually supposed to be the favourite food of the Hindu god Gorakshnath, whose idol is installed in a temple in Gorakhanth. The deity is served khichdi as an offering on the day of Makar Sankranti. There are a total of 320 calories in khichdi in a single serving (weighing 260 g). Of these calories, 19% is provided by protein, 68% by carbohydrates, and 13% by fat. In total, khichdi calories provide 16% of the daily calories needed by an adult eating a 2000 calorie diet.

In Ayurvedic traditions, khichdi is considered a sattvic food, which means it promotes purity, calmness, and harmony of the body and mind. It is often recommended during periods of fasting, cleansing, and recovery because it is easy to digest and nourishing. The combination of rice and lentils provides a complete protein, while the spices used in its preparation, such as turmeric and cumin, offer anti-inflammatory and digestive benefits.

Regional Variations


While khichdi is traditionally made with rice and lentils, its preparation varies widely across different regions of India, each adding its own unique touch


  • North India: In regions like Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, khichdi is often prepared with moong dal (split green gram) and served with ghee, curd, and papad.
  • Gujarat: Gujarati khichdi is often made with a mixture of rice and lentils, and sometimes even vegetables. It is typically served with kadhi, a yogurt-based curry.
  • South India: In Tamil Nadu, a version called "Pongal" is popular, especially during the Pongal festival. It is made with rice, moong dal, black pepper, and ghee.
  • Bengal: In West Bengal, a special version called "Bhoger Khichuri" is prepared as an offering to the deities during Durga Puja. It includes spices and vegetables, making it rich and flavorful.
